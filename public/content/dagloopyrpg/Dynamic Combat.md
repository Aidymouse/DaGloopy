# Dynamic Combat: A mix of OSR and PBTA

Creatures, you're surrounded. Goblins on all sides. Blades flash as they encroach. It's all or nothing. Now... roll initiative! Okay. Okay yeah. Okay what did you get? Okay 14. Wait I also got 14, who goes first? Okay what's your Dex..?

I have a problem. Turn based combat is really boring. It takes too long and it's not exciting. And yet, it's got some advantages that I just can't deny.

 - It's easy to run: Dead simple. Takes very little effort to go around in a circle.
 - By it's very nature, it ensures everyone spends some time in the spotlight.

"Dynamic" combat means things are changing often and unexpectedly. Turn based combat is the antithesis to this. Things change slowly and very routinely. This is a benefit for games where tactics are at their heart, but I just do not find that fun.

(Sidenote: This *can* happen in turn based games. Notably Draw Steel achieves this through the use of its mechanics, rather than in spite of them. The director resource Malice shifts the combat in unexpected ways, and it happens quite often. Still, it's at a slower pace than an action scene. In this case it's okay because the game is about that. But I do not want a game that is *about* slaying monsters, I want one that is *about* exploring hidden places, with combat being a fraught challenge element within it.)

The solution seems to be a looser style of combat akin to PBTA games, or even Daggerheart. 

The benefits are:
 - Combat is unpredicatble: A third option is added to task resolution - the mixed success. Since this is the most likely option, combat often changes in ways that cannot be predicted.
 - It's easy to be cinematic: No "you have to sit still while you get hit", it's always a set up and a player action. This feels less constraining than a turn, and keeps the action moving.

But this has other problems:
 - It doesn't work without mixed success
 - Monsters are solely reactive
 - Players feel left out
 - Energy Expenditure

## It doesn't work without mixed success
All things being equal, I actually like D&D style role playing games. I like one figurehead who's in charge of the setting, and I like that players explore that space, rather than having greats amount of input. Games of this ilk often have binary pass/fail mechanics as they fall more in line with this style of authoratative god figure taken on by the GM.

With no mixed success mechanic, combat struggles to be dynamic, because, though no one knows whether an attack will succeed or fail, we all know that it will *either* succeed or fail. And in most cases, an attack doesn't actually change anything other than lowering hit points. That is not a significant enough change to evoke the dynamic feeling.

It's not the mixed success itself that is the catalyst for dynamism, it's the fact that it forces *something else* to happen other than the normal effects of the attack.

### Monsters are Solely Reactive
Some part of my brain still requires monsters to have agency in ways similar to player characters. I don't like in PBTA games how enemy action is always only ever resisted by player characters. In the narrative sense, it doesn't change anything, but I like the emotional ride as a player of being luckily missed, rather than luckily rolling a successful dodge.

### Players Feel Left Out
When I'm resonsible for managing the spotlight, the action tends to focus on a cluster of characters right in the thick of things. It's actually kind of difficult to create action scenes where 6 characters all have a lot to do!

Quiet players, or even players sitting further away from the referee, tend to do less, because their voices are not heard. The main benefit of the turn structure is it automatically alots everyone their time.

### Energy Expenditure
One thing I don't like about PBTA games is that their dynamism comes at the expense of the energy expenditure of mixed success systems. The reason they can be exciting is because the referee is a human and can think of ways the scene can change. But this is taxing. Conjuring up what it looks like to "succeed at a cost" wears on me after a session of play time.

## My Take on the Solution

Core Mechanic: To achieve something, roll equal to or below a characters attribute (STR, DEX, CON, etc. The usual lot.). If you do, the action succeeds. Otherwise, the GM will apply one of the following complications:
    - The action fails
    - It comes at a cost

During a fight, an additional complication comes with a failed roll:
    - An enemy acts.

Combat happens in rounds. During one round, each character has two turns. A turn encompasses a movement and an action. Characters act in any order. The round ends once all player characters have taken both turns.

Every action, no matter how minor, has a roll. In many cases, the action itself cannot fail, but the roll determines if it can be achieved leaving enough room for an ally to act. On a failed roll, in addition to the action failing or coming at a cost, and enemy gets to act. An enemy action is like a player turn - they can move and take one action.

When a monster rolls to take an action, there is no succeess or failure, only soft hits and hard hits.

If a monster fails a roll for an action, it's not that it fails, it's just that it's set up. If the monster gets another turn this round, they do not roll, the follow through on that action. Unless someone else has changed the situation. It is possible (and encouraged) for a monsters "soft setup" to be ruined by the intervention of another character (or even the same characters second turn).

Monsters do not have action limits, and can act as many times in a round as players fail.

When a player is out of actions, they cannot do any more this round until next round. The round concludes when all players have taken all their turns.

At the end of the round, any monsters that have not yet acted may do so.

### Finer Points
"Well, I'm just going to move away!" - If a character is the target of a soft action, it requires an action for them to move away from it.

## Counter Arguments
*"Just get good at spotlight management! Pay attention to your quiet players, or tell them to speak up."*

This is a valid point, but the energy expenditure goes up, and I'm just not that smart. A systemic solution is a better fit for me.

*"So monsters in this system can't miss? Doesn't that go against your idea the monsters agency? Why have them roll at all?"*

Good point. I considered making it that monsters still hit or miss, it's just that the monsters first turn in a round were hard hits, and successes on subsequent turns are soft hits. I think this drags the round out into two seperate phases though (one where the monsters are ready to hit hard, and another where they can't do as much damage.) This is tactically interesting, but slows the round down.

I think monsters still exert agency. They can hit, and that hit is now true, without intervention. It's just that sometimes they leave a bit of room first. It is a compromise between absolute authority and dynamism that I'm comfortable with.
